About

Applied economist, university teacher, and researcher of ageing, labour and development

A research career built at the intersection of academic economics, applied econometrics and commercial analysis.

I am an applied economist based in Adelaide, South Australia, working on questions in labour economics, the economics of ageing, education and human capital, and development. My research uses applied econometrics — fixed-effects models, instrumental variables (IV-2SLS, FE-IV), difference-in-differences, event-study designs and regression discontinuity — to identify causal relationships in large longitudinal datasets, including SHARE, HILDA, ALSWH and JLMPS.

I completed a Ph.D. in Economics at Flinders University in 2025, with a thesis on Ageing with Purpose: Factors Shaping Well-being and Behaviour in Older Lives. Before returning to academic research, I spent six years working across industry and academia, including a commercial role as an Economic and Energy Market Analyst at Mubadala in Qatar, where I produced economic and energy-market analysis to support business decision-making under tight time constraints. That experience shaped how I now approach academic work: I treat a well-specified econometric model as only half the task — the other half is communicating what it means for the people who will use it, whether that is a journal reader, a policy audience or a commercial stakeholder.

I currently hold teaching academic appointments at Adelaide University and at the College of Business, Government and Law, Flinders University. At Adelaide University I lead the Innovation Academy in Digital Business, a partnership between Accenture and the university, where I design and deliver training in Agile, DevOps and Design Thinking and serve as academic lead for course redevelopment. I am also a Junior Fellow at Netspar and an Affiliate Researcher at the Global Labor Organization.

My published and ongoing work spans occupational variation in retirement effects on functional independence, education as a determinant of caregiving and daily-living outcomes, locus of control and wellbeing in later life, and household-level questions in development economics using Jordanian survey data. A parallel stream of working papers examines aged-care need, functional decline and retirement among Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander adults and older Australians, using HILDA and ALSWH.

Across research, teaching and industry work, I rely daily on Python, R and Stata for econometric modelling, and use large language models as part of my regular workflow for code development, literature synthesis and data preparation — always with critical review of outputs against research-grade standards. I am a full Australian work rights holder, available for Canberra-based work, and welcome conversations with academic, government, consulting and policy audiences about collaboration.

How I work

Evidence before narrative

Every applied result is checked against identification strategy, data quality and robustness before it is written up — the story follows the evidence, not the other way around.

Communicate for the audience in front of me

The same finding is written differently for a journal referee, a government policy team and a commercial stakeholder — the economics stays rigorous, the framing changes.

Longitudinal data, used properly

Panel and repeated cross-sectional survey data (SHARE, HILDA, ALSWH, JLMPS) reward careful handling of attrition, measurement and timing — that care is built into the modelling choices, not added afterwards.
